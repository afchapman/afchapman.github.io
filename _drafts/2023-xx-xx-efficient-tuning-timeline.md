In this blog post I'm going to take you through the timeline of a single project, from the initial ideas that motivated the work, through the stages of various experiments, the writing and submissions to journals, and ultimately the accepted manuscript.
The goal of this post is to highlight aspects of the scientific process that I think are important to understand, but not explicitly visible in the final output (that is, the article that is published in a scientific journal).
I'll describe some of my thought process at different stages through the timeline of the project, and discuss some of the issues that arose too - not all of these issues are applicable to every scientific project, but many are.
If you have never published a scientific article before, I hope this post helps to explain some of the complexities of scientific practice. But even if you have published many papers, and have run into the same problems as me many times before, hopefully this serves as a reminder that we're all in this together and perhaps encourages discussions that could help remove some of the barriers to publication that early career scientists face.

The project that I'm going to take you through here is one that was recently accepted for publication! The article is titled "Efficient tuning of attention to narrow and broad ranges of task-relevant feature values" and is published in *Visual Cognition* - you can access the article [here](https://dx.doi.org/10.1080/13506285.2023.2192993) if you have an instituional affiliation, or feel free to [email me](mailto:{{site.email}}) if you do not.
The specifics of the paper itself are not important for reading this blog post, and I'll try to provide the relevant context whenever I make direct reference to anything from the paper.

## Writing up and manuscript submission

By the end of Spring quarter 2021, we felt confident that we had collected sufficient data to put together an initial manuscript for submission for publication. The timing of this worked well, heading into the Summer break where I could focus on writing without the other grad school distractions of classes, TAing, and meetings. 

I wrote the first full draft of the paper by the start of July. Fortunately, I had already written pretty solid versions of the Methods and Results for all the in-lab experiments I conducted through 2019 and 2020, along with the outline of an Introduction. That meant that most of what I had left to do was put together sections for the online experiments, and complete the main arguments that would make up the Introduction and Discussion sections. And by this point, I was pretty familiar with the relevant literature.

Over the following few months, Viola and I went back and forth a number of times, refining that initial draft into something we felt was ready for submission to a journal. Through this process, the Introduction and Discussion were greatly expanded; I have a tendency in my writing to sometimes underexplain the rationale or logic that underlies the experiments, which likely comes down to having spent so much time working on the project and thinking through the details exhaustively - it's important to always take a step back and remember that your reader (even someone as deeply ingrained in the same literature as you are) doesn't necessarily know all those details, and that explaining your decisions and including the relevant justifications is a crucial part of publishing, if you ever want to make it past the peer-review process.

By September 17th 2021, we had the first version of the paper ready for submission.

### Journal 1

NB: I won't name the specific journals that we submitted to, since that's not the point of this post. All the journals we tried primarily publish experimental cognitive psychology research, and our submission order roughly proceeded from journals with a "broader" audience to increasingly more specific journals within our subfield.

* Initial submission: 09/17/2021
* Initial editor response: 12/23/2021
* Editor's decision: Major revision

We received reviews from two anonymous reviewers (a third had agreed, but did not submit their review). Generally, the reviews thought our manuscript was interesting and contained some good experiments, but were not completely convinced of our arguments. They also raised concerns that the manuscript was not appropriately suited for the audience of the journal (i.e., "not of broad enough interest").



* Revision submitted: 03/02/2022
* Editor's response: 05/31/2022
* Editor's decision: Reject

Unfortunately, our revision had not done enough to convince the reviewers or the editor that the paper was acceptable for publication. This was, of course, disappointing to us. But given that it was our first attempt, and to a journal that was more lucrative (and thus can accept a smaller proportion of the papers it receives), we were happy to have received two rounds of reviewer comments that we could use to improve the manuscript for the next journal we tried.

This second round of comments came from both of the previous reviewers and one new reviewer. This is a pretty common occurence, particularly if one of the initial reviewers had fewer concerns with the paper, or if the editor thinks they need a new perspective to help make a decision.
In our case, the new reviewer was pretty positive overall, but mostly seemed to think our experiments mostly scratched the surface of the topic, without really providing a complete answer to the questions we posed. This was a relatively fair point, from my point of view, but also didn't seem like a critical issue since it was partially the point we were trying to make (and justify future research on the topic).
