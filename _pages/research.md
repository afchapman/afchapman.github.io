My research is focused on understanding the neural mechanisms of visual perception and attention, in particular how attention is constrained by the structure of the visual system and the organization of perceptual representations over which it operates.

---
### Units of attentional selection

![image-placeholder](/assets/images/image-alignment-300x200.jpg){: .align-left}
How do we attend to different parts of the world? Different theories emphasize the role of specific mechanisms, be they spatial, object-based, or feature-based. How do these mechanisms constrain each other and in what situations is one prioritized above others? In current experiments, for example, we assess how attention to one feature of an object modulates processing of other, irrelevant features.
{: .text-justify}

### Perceptual organization and attention

![image-placeholder](/assets/images/image-alignment-300x200.jpg){: .align-right}
How does attention shape visual processing, not just the specific feature attended (e.g., the color red) but the changes that occur throughout an entire feature space. For example, how does attending to red items influence the processing of orange items relative to blue items? I am attempting to answer this question using a combination of behavioral psychophysics, in combination with neural measures of perceptual processes (using EEG and fMRI) and computational models of feature spaces.
{: .text-justify}

### Models of feature-based attention

![image-placeholder](/assets/images/image-alignment-300x200.jpg){: .align-left}
Traditionally, attention has been studied primarily in the spatial domain, with its own theories and models. What aspects of attention to visual features (e.g., color, motion direction, orientation) work similarly? For example, can we flexibly adjust the breadth of feature-based attention as required by task demands? Can we divide feature-based attention among different target features, or across feature dimensions?
{: .text-justify}
