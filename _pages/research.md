My research interests broadly cover topics in visual perception and attention, particularly in their neural mechanisms and how they impact our visual experience. You can read about a few different directions below.

### Units of attentional selection

![image-placeholder](/assets/images/image-alignment-300x200.jpg){: .align-left}

How do we attend to different parts of the world? Different theories emphasize the role of specific mechanisms, be they spatial, object-based, or feature-based. How do these mechanisms constrain each other and in what situations is one prioritized above others?

### Feature-based attention

![image-placeholder](/assets/images/image-alignment-300x200.jpg){: .align-right}

We can select specific visual information is by attending to particular relevant features (e.g., color, orientation, direction of motion). I am interested in the way feature-based attention shapes visual processing, not just the specific feature attended (e.g., the color red) but the changes that occur throughout an entire feature space. For example, how does attending to red items influence the processing of orange items relative to blue items?

---
### Perceptual cycles

![image-placeholder](/assets/images/image-alignment-300x200.jpg){: .align-left}

In a different line of research, I have been investigating the cyclical nature of perception. Previous research has shown that the ability to detect visual stimuli fluctuates across an interval of roughly 100 ms. What neural mechanisms are responsible for these cycles, and what is their purpose in the flow of information processing? Does individual variation in the length of these cycles reflect differences in how we experience the world around us?
